Meter Top Trumps: which meter survives MHHS?

June 2026 · 7 min read + a game

Look at the meter on the side of your house and you're looking at a fossil record. Whatever box happens to be on your wall belongs to a lineage stretching back more than a century — and each generation added a trick the one before it couldn't manage.

This is the story of how the electricity meter evolved: from a spinning aluminium disc that could only count, to a connected device that knows exactly what you used in every half-hour of the day. It's a story of meters slowly gaining new senses — first a counter, then a clock, then a remote signal, then a memory and finally a phone line home.

And it's a story that has suddenly come to a head, because of a quiet reform called MHHS that rewards the meters at the smart end of the family tree and quietly retires the ones at the other. So naturally, I turned the whole family into a game of Top Trumps.

Six meters tell the entire story. Here they are, oldest first — tap any one to see what it added to the lineage.

Six generations on the wall

Why the latest step suddenly matters: MHHS

For most of that century, the industry only ever knew your total consumption — so when it came to billing, it mostly guessed when you used it. Your usage got smeared across the day using a standard "profile": a national average of what a typical home does at a typical hour. A reasonable guess, but a guess all the same.

MHHS — Market-wide Half-Hourly Settlement — replaces the guess with the real thing. It went live in September 2025 and is migrating homes through to 2027, after which every meter will be billed on what you actually used in each half-hour — "settled" on the real thing, in the industry’s word, instead of on an average.

Why does that matter to you? Half-hourly data is the plumbing behind time-of-use tariffs like Octopus Agile. It's what lets a supplier charge you less for running the dishwasher at 2pm on a windy afternoon and steer you away from the expensive, carbon-heavy early-evening peak. It rewards flexibility — and a grid full of flexible homes is a cheaper, greener grid.

There's just one catch: your meter has to keep up. A meter that can't produce a half-hourly read is a meter living on borrowed time. Which brings us, at last, to the cards.

So, what's the takeaway?

Run the family tree end to end and the pattern is clear: every generation added a sense the last one lacked — a counter, a clock, a remote signal, a memory, a phone line home — and MHHS is simply the moment the market finally catches up to what the newest meters can already do. The smarter and better-connected your meter, the brighter its future.

Britain is well down that road. Around 41 million smart and advanced meters are now installed — roughly 71% of all meters — and the goal is to reach effectively every home by 2030. If you've still got a traditional spinning-disc meter it works fine today, but it can't do half-hourly, so you'll stay on estimates while everyone else moves to smarter tariffs. If you've got an RTS meter the situation is genuinely urgent: the radio signal that operates it is being switched off region by region, finishing by the end of June 2026 — MHHS or no MHHS.

And if you've got a SMETS2 smart meter? You're not just surviving the transition — you're holding the card the whole reform was designed around. Half-hourly data out of the box, proper time-of-use tariffs unlocked, and a real shot at a cheaper, greener bill.

The house always wins, as they say. In this case, so does the half-hourly meter.
