Why local clean power couldn't find a local buyer (until P442)

June 2026 · 6 min read · interactive

Picture a solar farm in a field, and a factory across the road that hums away all day. The obvious thing would be for the field to sell its power straight to the factory — short trip, clean energy, everyone happy. For about twenty years, it basically couldn't. Not because of the wires or the technology, but because of the way Britain keeps score of who owes what for electricity.

The rules actually allowed the idea. A small generator is permitted to supply a nearby customer directly without becoming a fully licensed energy company — this is called licence-exempt supply. The problem was the accounting. Every unit of electricity that moves through a meter gets swept into a national tallying system, and that system had no tidy way to say "this bit was local, and that bit came off the grid." So the local bit got lumped in with everything else — and picked up national charges it arguably shouldn't have. That was usually enough to kill the deal.

A small, deeply unglamorous rule change called P442 fixed exactly this. It created a kind of referee whose job is to look, every half hour, at how much of the local generation was genuinely used on site at the same moment — and label just that slice as local, so it can skip the national charges. Suddenly the field-to-factory deal adds up. Flip the diagram below to see what changed.

Why half an hour?

Britain's electricity market keeps its books in half-hour slots — 48 of them a day. It's the heartbeat of the whole system: prices, balancing, who supplied what, all settled half hour by half hour. P442 borrows the same rhythm. For power to count as genuinely "used locally," it has to be both generated and used in the same half-hour slot. No banking the lunchtime sunshine to cover the evening rush.

So in any given slot, the local amount is just the smaller of the two numbers: what the panels made, and what the business actually used. Make 2 MWh and use 3, and 2 counts as local — the other 1 comes off the grid as normal. Make 2 and use only 1, and just 1 is local, with the spare exported. That "smaller of the two" is exactly what the matching agent works out, slot by slot.

What were these national charges, anyway?

Part of every electricity bill quietly funds national programmes. Two of the big ones: Contracts for Difference, which underwrite large renewable and nuclear projects, and the Capacity Market, which pays power stations and batteries to be on standby for tight winter evenings. Both are collected from licensed suppliers based on how much they sell, and passed through to bills.

They're sensible schemes. But a small solar farm selling to the business next door isn't really drawing on them for that local slice — so sweeping it up in those charges never quite made sense. P442 is, at heart, the mechanism that lets that slice off the hook. The saving can run to roughly £60 per MWh, which the generator and the buyer can share between them.

So what's an "ESNA"? It's the official name for the referee: an Exempt Supply Notification Agent. It has to be vetted and approved, and it replaces what used to be a slow, manual paperwork process — which is a big part of why this never scaled before. Think of it as an accredited scorekeeper that both sides, and the regulator, can trust.

Who actually gets to do this?

There are sensible limits, so this stays a genuinely local affair. It's aimed at small set-ups — up to 5MW of supply in total, with a cap on how much can go to homes rather than businesses. That's the scale of a community solar scheme, a local wind project, a big commercial rooftop, or a factory with a generator over the fence. It is emphatically not a route for a national supplier to dodge charges.

What it unlocks

For a community energy group, it turns a nice idea into something that can actually pay for itself. For a company chasing real clean-energy credentials — not just certificates bought in bulk, but power it can point to and say "that came from those panels, at the moment we used it" — it offers exactly that, with a timestamped trail to prove it. And for the small generator, it means a better price and a buyer on the doorstep, which makes the next local solar or wind project easier to justify building.

Where it stops

It's worth being clear about what P442 isn't. It's not peer-to-peer energy trading for every household, and it's not a loophole. The size limit and the half-hour matching test keep it tied to generation and demand that genuinely sit close together. Anything that isn't matched in the moment simply comes from your normal supplier, with the usual charges. The win is narrow but real: it clears away the accounting that kept local clean supply stuck as a good idea.

When did this happen?

The regulator, Ofgem, approved P442 in May 2024, and it went live through early 2025. The first matching agents were up and running over the course of 2025, and in early 2026 the government refreshed its guidance for small suppliers to match. None of it made headlines — but it's the quiet sort of plumbing change that decides whether local clean energy stays niche or starts to add up.
