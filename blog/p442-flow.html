P442, before and after: how licence-exempt supply finally adds up

June 2026 · 6 min read · interactive

For two decades, licence-exempt supply was a good idea the settlement system couldn't actually carry. The Class A exemption let a small generator supply a local consumer without a supply licence — but inside the Balancing and Settlement Code there was no clean way to split a single SVA metering system's volume into the part that was genuinely exempt and the part that was licensable. So the locally-matched volume sat in the same bucket as everything else, exposed to the supplier-borne charges that fund Electricity Market Reform.

BSC modification P442 — "Reporting chargeable volumes for exempt and licensed supply" — closes that gap. It introduces a qualified third-party role, the Exempt Supply Notification Agent (ESNA), that time-matches generation against consumption in each half-hour and reports the exempt volume into central settlement. The matched volume is then excluded from EMR levies — Contracts for Difference and the Capacity Market — and the economics of local supply finally close.

The mechanic: matched within the half-hour

The exemption is deliberately strict. Only electricity that is both generated and consumed within the same half-hourly settlement period counts as licence-exempt — there is no carrying forward, no daily netting, no notional matching across periods. In each settlement period the exempt volume is, in effect, the minimum of generation and demand behind the boundary, capped by the Class A threshold.

That is what the ESNA exists to compute. It validates the half-hourly generation and consumption data (from the HHDC or supplier), calculates the licensed-versus-exempt split, and submits the exempt volumes into central BSC systems so the EMR delivery body charges levies only on the licensed remainder. Crucially, an ESNA must go through a qualification process, which is what replaces the old manual, Elexon-administered handling and gives counterparties confidence the numbers are auditable.

Class A, briefly. The small-supplier exemption applies where total supply is no more than 5MW, of which no more than 2.5MW may go to domestic consumers. It is technology-neutral, needs no pre-approval (operators self-assess), and can sit alongside generation and distribution licence exemptions.

What it actually enables

Strip away the settlement plumbing and P442 does one commercially significant thing: it lets the locally-matched volume escape charges that only ever applied to licensed suppliers. On the avoided EMR levies alone — the CfD Supplier Obligation and the Capacity Market Supplier Charge, plus other supplier-borne policy costs — the saving can run to roughly £60/MWh, value that can be split between the generator and the offtaker.

That reshapes the route to market for a specific class of project. Sub-5MW solar, onshore wind, hydro and anaerobic digestion, commercial rooftop, community schemes, and private-wire or embedded sites can now contract directly with a business consumer through a corporate sleeved PPA without the exempt volume being penalised, and without the disproportionate reporting burden that previously deterred smaller players. For the generator it is a higher, time-stamped price; for the corporate buyer it is verifiable clean supply aligned to actual generation periods; for licensed suppliers it is a framework to integrate time-matching and settle the residual within existing contracts.

Where it stops

P442 is a settlement and reporting reform, not a new retail market. The exemption is bounded by the 5MW Class A limit and the half-hourly matching test, so it favours co-located or closely-coupled generation and demand rather than diffuse peer-to-peer trading across the network. Anything that isn't matched in-period is simply balanced through a normal retail supply contract, with the usual charges. The win is narrow but real: it removes the specific administrative and cost blockers that kept licence-exempt supply sub-scale.

Status

Ofgem approved P442 in May 2024, with implementation through the February 2025 BSC release; the first ESNAs were accredited over the course of 2025. In January 2026 DESNZ refreshed its Class A supply-exemption guidance to align with the change, clarifying the thresholds and confirming that operators self-assess against the statutory criteria under the Electricity Act 1989 and the Class Exemptions Order 2001. The combination — a working settlement route plus clearer guidance — is what moves licence-exempt, time-matched supply from niche to genuinely bankable.
